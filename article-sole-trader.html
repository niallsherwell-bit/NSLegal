Sole trader or limited company — which should you choose?

A plain-English guide to picking your business structure: what each one means for your liability, your tax bill, and your admin.

AreaCompany & Tax
Read5 min
JurisdictionEngland & Wales
Updated[Month 2026]

You've started earning from your own work — freelancing, contracting, or running a small venture — and now there's a decision to make: stay a sole trader, or set up a limited company? It sounds like dry paperwork, but the choice shapes who's on the hook if things go wrong, how much tax you pay, and how much admin you carry. Here's how to think about it.

The short answer

If you're just starting out and the risks are low, being a sole trader is simpler and cheaper. Once your profits grow or you're taking on real liability, a limited company usually makes more sense — for the legal protection, and often for the tax. The detail is below.

What is a sole trader?

A sole trader is the simplest way to run a business as an individual, without setting up a separate company. You and the business are legally the same thing. Picture a self-employed plumber who owns his own van and tools: he is the business, and he owns its assets personally.

That simplicity has a sting. As a sole trader, you're personally responsible for the business's contracts and debts. If you take out a business loan and can't repay it, your personal assets are at risk — not just your equipment, but potentially your car or your home, which can be sold under a court order through a trustee in bankruptcy (the person appointed to recover what's owed to creditors).

On tax, a sole trader pays income tax on their profits through Self Assessment, plus self-employed National Insurance, and the rate rises as you earn more. You'll need to register with HMRC and file a return each year once your trading income passes £1,000 — and file on time, to avoid penalties.

The upside is far fewer formalities. There's no need to register with Companies House, only with HMRC for Self Assessment. There's no minimum capital to hold beyond any security a lender asks for, and no constitutional documents to draw up before you can start trading.

What is a limited company?

A private company limited by shares is a separate legal entity that issues shares to its owners, the shareholders. Instead of being the business, you own a slice of it.

Its headline advantage is limited liability. Because the company is legally a person in its own right, debts and legal claims against it are generally recovered from the company's assets, not your personal ones. Your downside is, in principle, limited to what you've put in.

It can also make raising money easier. A private company can't sell shares to the public, but lenders often treat a company with identifiable assets as lower-risk than a sole trader, which can help with borrowing — though banks frequently ask the director (often you) to give a personal guarantee, which puts your own assets back on the line.

On tax, companies pay corporation tax: 19% on profits under £50,000 and 25% on profits over £250,000, with a marginal band between the two — generally lower than income tax rates. The company itself gets no personal allowance. But remember the second layer: when you take money out as salary or dividends, that's taxed in your hands under the income tax rules above.

The trade-off is admin. A company needs articles of association, a memorandum, annual accounts and ongoing filings. Setting one up is cheaper than people expect — around £50 online, with model articles and templates provided — but staying compliant, especially on tax, usually means paying for an accountant.

So what should you choose?

It depends on a few specific things, not just preference. Stage matters most: early on, when profits are modest and risks are low, the simplicity of being a sole trader often wins. As you grow — bigger contracts, more borrowing, more at stake — the protection and tax treatment of a company start to earn their keep.

Weigh four things: your profit level, how much personal risk you're exposed to, your appetite for admin, and how you want clients to see you. And when the stakes get real, it's worth getting tailored advice before you commit either way.
